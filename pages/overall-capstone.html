Overall Capstone

Setting the Scope

I decided to create a web-based collaborative diagram editor because it mixed three of my favorite interests in computer science: web development, UI/UX, and networking. Creating a website requires web development for technical functionality. User interface and user experience are the core components for the usability of any website. The collaboration feature requires the integration of networking.

I chose to make a diagram editor because it is a simple concept with a high amount of technical potential. I didn’t want to get sidetracked by an ambitious concept because I would have been more likely to get distracted with the concept itself rather than the technical component. A diagram editor is a beneficial technical challenge due to having to develop a front-end and back-end. A front-end is important for the graphic and visual demands of diagrams, and collaboration through networking requires a back-end.

Using the Double Diamond Methodology

I went into the project with the intention of using the double diamond design methodology. My goal was to follow a structured methodology to gain insight into what project management is like at an industry-level. Something I failed to consider was that the goal of a professional design methodology and the goal of the capstone are different. The goal of the former is to create a useful and viable product, while the capstone’s goal is to learn about development. The dissonance between goals created issues early on. I tried to steadily create output even when the output wasn’t useful to the project. After realizing that I wasn’t making much progress, I took a more balanced approach. I am now learning and practicing to balance creating output that is useful and learning what is necessary to create that output.

Another factor that contributed to the allure of the double diamond methodology was my desire for structure. Having structure gives me comfort and allows me to focus solely on the project itself. However, perfect structure is an unrealistic expectation and something I won’t encounter in the professional world. So, part of dropping the double diamond methodology was to force myself to become comfortable having to navigate an unfamiliar project space. It is not preferable, but becoming adaptable to real world situations is important to me and something I am improving at.

Prioritizing Functionality over Aesthetics

User interface and graphic design are two interests of mine. The design of websites is important for any product’s commercial success and user interest, so I wanted to spend time perfecting my website’s design. I planned to follow a waterfall-esque process for every webpage by sketching its wireframe, designing its outline in Figma, then implementing it in CSS. Only after I completed this process would I start technical development of the website.

The logic behind focusing on webpage design was sound. It aligned with my personal interests and would assist in the appeal of my website. However, I failed to consider the goal of the capstone project and my bachelor’s degree. The purpose of the project was to build and develop technical competencies in computer science. I was initially trying to create a website instead of trying to develop one.

I learned to prioritize the growth of my academics and career in balance with my personal interests. I shifted my perspective to align with the capstone project’s goal of technical development and growth. My new approach involved focusing on development while still making time for UI design. The two are not mutually exclusive, so I could work on them separately or integrate them when needed. The balance between development and design enables me to grow my skills in a sustainable and enjoyable way that ultimately benefits my academics and career.

Discovering the Correct Tool Late

Throughout development, I searched for and tested different tools to optimize the performance of my website. I tried HTML first, which I knew was not the best option, but I used it for prototyping purposes. I then researched and settled on React, confident in its capabilities for performance. However, during the last two weeks of the academic quarter, I learned through a professional software developer that React was not the best option. The developer explained that a tool called Canvas API is more performant than React. I was aware of Canvas API, but I dismissed it as an option early into the capstone project.

Before implementing Canvas API, I had the impression that it was only useful for 2D web-based games. I did not spend the time or effort to critically evaluate the tool, so I failed to consider all the use cases and capabilities of Canvas API. After more research into the tool, I discovered its performance capabilities regarding 2D graphics. I then realized why I only saw it used in web-based games. If many people use a particular tool for one purpose, that does not mean the tool is useful only for that one purpose. The experience taught me to critically evaluate the tools I encounter, since surface-level impressions are not reliable.

After learning about the effectiveness of Canvas API, I became discouraged because it took me eight weeks to discover the correct tool. If I had learned about and implemented Canvas API earlier, I would have made much more progress. After some reflection, I decided to move forward because that is all I could do. There is no benefit to regret. I also realized that it took making the wrong choice to make the correct choice. The reason why the delay in making the correct choice felt bad is because I viewed errors as avoidable when they are actually inevitable. I am learning to embrace setbacks as learning opportunities.

Conclusion

This capstone project is my first experience working on a large project that spans many months. It forced me to find a research and project methodology that aligns with the goals of the project and my own work style. The increased time scope of the project also increased the technical scope. The increased technical demand exposed me to working with many different tools that all interact with each other, creating a unique and complex development environment.

The conflict between perfection and progress was the biggest challenge for me throughout the capstone project. I like to do things the right way the first time. This is an unrealistic expectation, but one that sticks with me regardless. Through the motivation and requirements of the capstone project, I am making consistent efforts that break the perfection mindset. One example is me learning about Canvas API late. It pained me to go through that, but I learned valuable lessons from the experience and reinforced the idea that mistakes can be beneficial. I plan to continue making progress even if it causes mistakes, because each mistake is a lesson learned and a step away from the perfection mindset.

Regarding development, my next step for this capstone project is to complete the front-end functionality utilizing Canvas API. Line connections between shapes is the biggest challenge, which requires research into vector math. The last step is to implement the back-end to allow for collaboration. I want the final product to be satisfactory enough for me to use it in place of other professional diagram editors.
